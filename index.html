The Emotion Chain

The prompting event. Something happens — external (someone cancels on you) or internal (a memory surfaces). This is the trigger, not the emotion itself.

Someone doesn't text you back for hours.

Your interpretation. Your brain makes meaning instantly, usually without you noticing. This is where your history, beliefs, and assumptions live. The same event produces completely different emotions depending on the story your brain tells.

"They're ignoring me" vs. "They're probably busy" — same event, different chain.

The emotion shows up. Not just a feeling — a full-body experience. Emotions come with physical sensations, facial expressions, and cognitive changes. They happen fast and carry information.

Anxiety: chest tightens, mind races. Anger: jaw clenches, heat rises, focus narrows.

The action urge. Every emotion has a built-in urge. Fear says run. Anger says fight. Shame says hide. These urges are not choices — they're wired in. The choice comes next.

Anxiety urge: check your phone again, send a follow-up, rehearse what you'll say.

What you actually do. This is where skill meets emotion. You can follow the urge or choose differently. The goal isn't to always override the urge — sometimes it fits. The goal is to have a choice.

You notice the urge to double-text. You sit with it. Then you decide with your full brain, not just the anxious part.
Key concepts

The building blocks

Emotions have functions
+

Emotions are not problems to fix. They evolved because they're useful. Each one carries information and prepares you for action.

  • Fear protects you from danger
  • Anger signals a boundary has been crossed
  • Sadness signals loss and invites support
  • Guilt says you've violated your own values
  • Shame says you're at risk of rejection
The question is never "how do I stop feeling this?" — it's "what is this telling me, and does it fit what's actually happening?"
Check the facts
+

Before acting on an emotion, check whether it fits the facts. Not whether the emotion is real — it's always real. Whether it matches the situation.

  • What event triggered the emotion?
  • What am I telling myself about it?
  • Am I confusing an interpretation with a fact?
  • What's the actual threat level here?
An emotion can be real and not fit the situation. You can feel terrified in a safe room. Both things are true.
What is opposite action?
+

When an emotion doesn't fit the facts — or when acting on it will make things worse — you do the opposite of what the emotion pushes you to do. All the way.

  • Not faking — choosing a different behavioral path
  • All the way: body language, voice, posture, everything
  • Half-measures don't work — approaching what you fear while clenching your fists isn't opposite action
  • It works because emotions and actions reinforce each other — change one, the other follows
Opposite action is not suppression. Suppression says "don't feel that." Opposite action says "I feel this, and I'm choosing to act differently because the urge isn't helping me right now."
When to use it — and when not to
+

Opposite action is a specific tool for specific situations. It is not the answer to every emotion.

  • Use it when: the emotion doesn't fit the facts, or acting on the urge will make things worse
  • Don't use it when: the emotion fits and acting on the urge is effective
  • If your anger is about a real boundary violation — honor it
  • If your guilt is about something you actually did — that guilt is useful
The skill is knowing when the urge is helping you and when it's running on autopilot.
Opposite Action Reference

15–20 minThe urge vs. the opposite — by emotion

Left side is what the emotion pushes you to do. Right side is the opposite action — for when the emotion doesn't fit the facts or when acting on it makes things worse.

The urge
The opposite
Fear / Anxiety
Avoid, escape, run, freeze. Get away from the thing.
Approach
Do what you're afraid of, all the way. Relax your body. Stay until the fear drops on its own.
Anger
Attack, yell, shut down, punish. Make them pay.
Gently avoid or be kind
Step back. Unclench your hands. Speak softly. Do something small that's kind.
Sadness
Withdraw, isolate, stay in bed, give up.
Get active, reach out
Move your body. Contact someone. Do one thing even if it feels pointless.
Shame
Hide, disappear, avoid eye contact, keep the secret.
Share what you're hiding
Tell someone you trust. Make eye contact. Let yourself be seen. Shame loses power when spoken.
Guilt (when unjustified)
Apologize excessively, punish yourself, overcompensate.
Let it go
Stop apologizing. Don't overpromise. Remind yourself the guilt doesn't match the situation.
Disgust (toward self)
Push away, reject, create distance.
Move toward, stay close
Don't push away. Stay present. Approach instead of recoiling.
Guided exercise

Check the facts

Think of an emotion you've been sitting with this week. Walk through these steps — in your head, out loud, or on paper.

1
What emotion am I feeling?
Name it specifically. "Bad" isn't an emotion. Angry, ashamed, scared, hurt — those are.
2
What happened that triggered it?
Just the facts. What would a camera have seen?
3
What am I telling myself about it?
This is the interpretation layer. What story is your brain adding?
4
Does the emotion fit the facts?
Is the threat real? Is the loss real? "Yes" and "no" are both fine.
5
What does the emotion want me to do?
Name the urge. What is it pushing you toward?
6
Will acting on it help or make things worse?
If it helps — follow the urge. If not — opposite action.

You don't have to share any of this. The exercise works in your head. If something comes up you want to bring to the group, you can.
